Our Planet

Earth, our home planet. The third planet from the sun, with a radius of 3,959 miles. Earth is the fifth largest planet in our solar system and the only confirmed place in the univerese to host life.

Our Home

Our home is a complex planet dependent on multiple ecosystems, from the biosphere to the microhabitats we are all connected and bound with a responsibility to contribute to a better world to leave behind.

Make an impact

Impact is a big word, but not necessarily a big action.

  • Turn the tap of when your brushing your teeth.
  • Go vegetarian for a day, not only for the sake of the planet but also your health.
  • Plant a tree.

The future

Future changes are expected to include a warmer atmosphere, a warmer and more acidic ocean, higher sea levels, and larger changes in precipitation patterns. The extent of future climate change depends on what we do now to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The more we emit, the larger future changes will be.

Equinox

An equinox is an event in which a planet's subsolar point passes through its Equator. The equinoxes are the only time when both the Northern and Southern Hemisphere experience roughly equal amounts of daytime and nighttime.

Comet Pons-Brooks

The colossal comet, 12P/Pons-Brooks, three times larger than Mount Everest, is speeding toward the inner solar system. Comprising ice, dust, and gas, the cryovolcanic comet surprised astronomers with multiple 2023 outbursts, rapidly brightening. As it approaches the sun in March, gravitational forces will accelerate its pace, and by April, it might be visible to the naked eye just after sunset in the western sky. Keep an eye out for this celestial spectacle on April 12.

Comet C/2023 A3 (Tsuchinshan-ATLAS)

Comet chasers are eagerly tracking A3 Tsuchinshan-ATLAS, discovered in February 2023, set to dazzle in late 2024. Visible with small telescopes by early summer, it approaches the sun and Earth by September, potentially brightening for naked-eye visibility in southern latitudes. If it survives, prime viewing in the Northern Hemisphere starts around October 12, promising a captivating celestial display as it ascends each night. Despite the challenge of predicting comet behavior, early signs suggest Tsuchinshan-ATLAS could be a remarkable addition to our night skies.

The ringed planet joins the moon

From September to December, celestial enthusiasts can witness a captivating series of lunar-Saturn convergences. Commencing on September 17 and recurring on October 14 and 15, November 11, and December 8, these visually striking events will unfold just after sunset. Visible to the naked eye, the moon and Saturn, with their contrasting colors, will create an impressive spectacle, perfect for observation with lower-magnification binoculars. While not close enough for telescopic viewing together, the silvery lunar glow beside Saturn's distinct yellow tint will be a celestial delight.
